The Problem of Heat

Hundreds of millions of people already face increasingly adverse climatic conditions. As wet-bulb temperatures rise, the risk moves from productivity loss to death, and existing solutions are failing the people who need them most.

1.0 Overview

The Scale of the Problem

Hundreds of millions of people already face dangerous wet-bulb temperatures across India and South Asia, from the Indo-Gangetic Plain to peninsular cities. The risks are not abstract: potentially lethal heat conditions are already being recorded regularly, and the economic consequences are already measurable.

⚠️

Why existing solutions fail

Many of the people most exposed to extreme heat live in regions with per-capita GDPs under $5,000 USD, limiting individual and organizational resources for heat management. Additionally, all conventional cooling confines workers to air-conditioned spaces, making outdoor labor infeasible during heat events.

1.1 Human Physiology

How Heat Kills

People are ill-equipped to thrive or survive in intense heat. Animals have no natural refrigeration cycle. They rely on evaporation (the latent heat of vaporization) for cooling. The standard metric, Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT), accounts for air temperature, humidity, solar radiation, and wind: all variables driving heat transfer into and out of the body.

≥25°C
Caution

Strenuous work begins to risk heat-related illness, even in healthy individuals.

≥30.5°C
High Risk

Young, healthy subjects could not carry out simple tasks mimicking daily activities in controlled experiments.

≥32°C
Danger

Even short periods of work by a healthy individual risk death. This is our primary target WBGT for product efficacy.

≥35°C
Lethal

Sustained heavy physical work (e.g. heavy lifting) becomes acutely life-threatening. Even stationary individuals face severe heat stroke risk within hours.

Heat risk can manifest progressively as dizziness, nausea, vomiting, fainting, and heatstroke before reaching life-threatening levels.

1.3 India Focus

India: Extreme Heat and Outdoor Work

India combines high solar load, long hot seasons, and a very large share of outdoor and informal work. In many northern and central districts, daytime WBGT routinely exceeds 30–32°C during the pre-monsoon and monsoon months — levels at which safe work capacity for moderate to heavy tasks collapses. Studies estimate that India loses over 100–250 billion labour hours each year to heat and humidity across agriculture, construction, and manufacturing.

⚠️

Heat Advisories Are Not Enough

Many Indian states and cities issue heat advisories or adjust school and shift timings during heatwaves. But heat risk is driven by humidity, radiant load, and task intensity in addition to air temperature. Conditions can become hazardous well outside official "heatwave" days, and risk varies dramatically between shaded sites and exposed worksites.

Why Current Protections Fall Short

📅 Seasonal spread
 Dangerous heat now extends from March through October in many regions, well beyond traditional "summer" months.
🕛 Workday timing
 Heat stress often peaks mid- to late-afternoon, but dangerous WBGT levels can already be reached by late morning when outdoor work is in full swing.
🌡️ Static thresholds
 Simple temperature cutoffs ignore humidity, solar radiation, workload, and clothing — the factors that actually determine physiological heat strain.
💧 Humidity variability
 Coastal and monsoon-affected regions experience rapid humidity spikes that dramatically increase WBGT without raising dry-bulb temperature.
1.4 Economic Argument

The Cost of Heat to Business

Labor productivity (both physical and intellectual) decreases drastically with increasing heat. The loss of even 10–15% of output across hundreds of millions of person-days represents a significant economic impact for the businesses and sectors involved.

📉

Productivity Collapse

At WBGT 32°C, workers performing 500 W of physically demanding work (e.g. heavy lifting or operating manual tools) lose up to 50% of sustainable output. Even stationary 200 W tasks see >25% losses at these temperatures.

💰

Injury Costs

A single heat exhaustion workplace injury costs an employer approximately ₹71 lakh on average (greenTEG data), including medical, lost time, and liability, making preventive cooling economically rational.
